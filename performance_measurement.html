Performance Measurement and KPIs

To ensure the strategy stays on track and delivers the intended results, OMSB will implement a **robust performance measurement and evaluation framework**. This framework will use a set of Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) aligned with both OMSB’s strategic objectives and the national Digital Transformation Excellence criteria. Regular monitoring of these KPIs will enable OMSB leadership to gauge progress, make data-driven adjustments, and demonstrate success to stakeholders. The key elements of the performance management approach are:

Alignment with National Excellence Framework

OMSB’s KPIs are mapped to the Government’s three perspectives of digital excellence – **Digital Services Maturity, Institutional Readiness, and Actual Impact**. This ensures OMSB is tracking what matters not just internally but also in the eyes of national evaluators. For the Digital Services Maturity perspective (which often accounts for ~30% of the score), OMSB will track indicators such as - **Service Catalog Completion** (target 100% by 2026); **Process Simplification** (aiming for 100% by 2031, with interim targets like ~30% by 2026 and ~70% by 2029); **Service Automation** (target 100% by 2031, intermediate 50% by 2028); **Multi-channel Availability** (goal - all major services on at least web and mobile by 2027); **User Satisfaction** (targeting ≥90% satisfaction by 2031); **Service Usage Rate** (target ~90% online usage by 2031); and **Continuous Improvement** (number of service improvements implemented annually based on user feedback). These KPIs mirror the key facets of service maturity - coverage, quality, automation, accessibility, user experience, and improvement frequency.

Institutional Readiness & Efficiency KPIs

For the Institutional Readiness perspective (~40% weight in the framework), OMSB will monitor metrics that reflect internal capacity and governance. These include - **Strategy Implementation Progress** (>90% on-time delivery each year); **IT Governance Maturity** (“managed” or “optimized” level by 2031); **Compliance Score** (100% compliance by 2027); **Business Continuity Readiness** (RTO fulfillment in drills by 2028); **Enterprise Architecture Maturity** (high maturity by 2029); **Workforce Digital Capacity** (% of staff who have completed advanced digital training - target 50% by 2031); **Change Management Metrics** (upward trend in staff openness); **Data Analytics Usage** (all key decisions to be data-informed by 2031); **Project Delivery Performance** (>85% adherence, improving to >95% by 2032); **Advanced Technology Adoption** (# of AI/emerging tech solutions in production); **Operational Efficiency Gains** (improved trainee-to-admin staff ratio); **Integration with Shared Platforms** (all relevant national platform integrations by 2027); and **Security Posture** (e.g. number of significant security incidents - target zero breaches). These indicators will demonstrate that OMSB is building the internal muscle (people, processes, tech infrastructure) to sustain digital transformation.

Actual Impact & Outcomes KPIs

The Actual Impact perspective (~30% weight) looks at the tangible results of digital transformation on stakeholders and organizational outcomes. OMSB will measure - **Service Simplification/Automation Outcomes** (meet/exceed annual goals); **Integration Benefits** (data exchange volume, reduction in manual verifications); **Beneficiary Satisfaction** (aiming for an upward trend to ≥90% by 2031); **Data Sharing Readiness** (open data readiness by 2029); **Cost Savings/Avoidance** (target to realize significant cumulative savings by 2031); and **Outcome Improvements** (linking digital efforts to core mission outcomes, e.g., improvement in exam pass rates, reduction in time to fill residency slots, improved accreditation review times). These speak to how digital transformation has improved the end results that matter – such as quality of education, service quality, and stakeholder trust.

Digital Transformation Scorecard

OMSB will compile all these KPIs into a **Digital Transformation Scorecard** that will be reviewed **quarterly** by the DT Steering Committee and annually by top leadership. This scorecard will essentially mirror the government’s evaluation but with more granular internal metrics. It will use **traffic-light indicators** (green/yellow/red) to show progress against targets for each KPI. The Steering Committee will use this to identify lagging areas and mandate corrective actions. The scorecard ensures accountability – if a metric is off-track, there’s clear visibility and impetus to address it. By aligning it with the national excellence criteria, OMSB also keeps itself prepared for formal evaluations (essentially doing a self-assessment continuously).

Monitoring Tools and Processes

Many KPIs will be tracked through the new digital systems themselves, reducing manual data collection. OMSB plans to implement a central **KPI Dashboard by 2027** that automatically pulls live data from various systems (portal analytics, service management systems, etc.) for near-real-time monitoring. In addition, OMSB will produce an **Annual Digital Transformation Report** starting in 2026, summarizing progress, KPI achievements, challenges, and priorities. This report will be submitted to OMSB’s Board and shared with the national digital transformation program office. Furthermore, OMSB will actively participate in any government-run assessments or audits, ensuring evidence is prepared for each required indicator. The organization’s aim is not to “chase a score” but to ensure that meeting those indicators is a byproduct of genuinely improved performance.

Stakeholder Feedback and Adaptation

Quantitative KPIs will be complemented by **qualitative feedback mechanisms**. OMSB will establish **continuous feedback loops** with its stakeholders - for example, embedding post-service satisfaction surveys, conducting an annual survey of residents/fellows, and organizing focus groups or user councils. Metrics like **Net Promoter Score (NPS)** might be tracked to gauge overall sentiment. A reduction in certain types of complaints will be a sign of success, while recurring complaints will trigger a root cause analysis. By Phase 2, OMSB could even employ **sentiment analysis** on social media to pick up external perceptions. Regular stakeholder input ensures OMSB remains responsive and user-focused, and provides early warning of issues not captured by internal metrics.

Benefits Realization Tracking

For each major project under the strategy, OMSB will define **expected benefits at the start** (e.g. “reduce application processing from 30 days to 10 days”) and then measure after implementation to confirm if the benefit was achieved. This practice of **benefits realization** ensures the strategy delivers real-world value, not just system deployments. If a benefit falls short – say the average processing time is still 15 days instead of the targeted 10 – OMSB will investigate why (perhaps users aren’t using the system correctly, or maybe an external dependency still causes delay) and address it. Benefits tracking will be part of the project close-out reports and will be overseen by the Steering Committee as well. This disciplined approach ties the strategy activities back to the outcomes that matter (time savings, cost savings, quality improvements) and helps course-correct if a project isn’t delivering as expected.

Monitoring & Governance

Scorecard & Review Cadence

  • Digital Transformation Scorecard: Reviewed Quarterly by the Steering Committee and Annually by top leadership.
  • Central KPI Dashboard: Implementation planned by 2027 to pull near-real-time data automatically.
  • Annual Report: Summarized progress report produced Annually starting 2026 for the Board and national program office.

Feedback & Adaptation Mechanisms

  • Continuous Feedback Loops: Post-service surveys, annual stakeholder surveys, and dedicated user councils/focus groups.
  • Benefits Realization Tracking: Expected benefits are defined at project start, and measured at close-out to confirm value delivery.
  • Complaint Analysis: Reduction in complaints is a success metric; recurring complaints trigger root cause analysis for immediate fix.

Compass and Dashboard

By implementing this comprehensive performance management approach, OMSB ensures **transparency and accountability** throughout the transformation. The leadership will have visibility at all times into what’s working and what’s not, enabling proactive management. Moreover, by aligning with national KPIs and continuously measuring progress, OMSB positions itself to achieve high scores in formal digital government evaluations – but more importantly, to truly realize the promised improvements in service delivery and institutional capacity. As this is a long-term transformation, these monitoring mechanisms serve as the **compass and dashboard** that keep OMSB guided toward its 2035 targets, ensuring the journey yields real benefits at every stage.
