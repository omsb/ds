Integration with National and Regional Platforms

A key principle of OMSB’s digital strategy is **“integration, not isolation.”** OMSB will actively connect its systems with national and regional digital platforms to ensure seamless data flow, improve services for users, and contribute to broader government objectives of unified e-government. Integration prevents duplication, enhances verification capabilities, and extends OMSB’s reach. The strategy outlines several critical integration initiatives:

Ministry of Health’s Nabd Al Shifa Electronic Health Record (EHR)

By **2027**, OMSB will establish a secure, two-way integration with the MoH’s central EHR system (**Nabd Al Shifa**). This integration will allow OMSB to pull relevant clinical data to inform training and accreditation, and push OMSB data to the health system. For example, OMSB can link residents’ training logbooks or case logs to actual patient cases/procedures in the EHR, providing more robust evidence of clinical exposure. Conversely, when a doctor completes OMSB training and is certified, that qualification info can flow into the EHR/HR systems of MoH for workforce deployment. Training site evaluations can be enhanced by EHR data (e.g. case volumes at a hospital to ensure residents get enough experience). Technically, this will likely use standardized health data exchange formats (**HL7/FHIR APIs**) to send and receive data in real-time while maintaining patient privacy. A concrete example outcome - An MoH hospital administrator could look up a specialist in their HR system and see that the person’s OMSB certification is verified and up-to-date, thanks to the integration. Such integration strengthens workforce planning and ensures OMSB’s credentialing is instantly recognized in the health service.

National Unified Government Services Portal

By the end of **2026**, OMSB will onboard its public-facing services onto Oman’s **unified eServices portal**. This is the “one-stop shop” portal where citizens and residents can find all government services. For OMSB, this means publishing all its service information (eligibility, process, documents needed, fees, etc.) on the unified portal and providing links or integration so that users can access OMSB’s online services from that central portal. Additionally, OMSB will enable **Single Sign-On** via the national digital identity (e.g. using the **Tam platform** or PKI-based ID) so that users can log into OMSB’s services with their national eID credentials. This significantly improves convenience – a user managing various government transactions uses one account and doesn’t have to remember separate OMSB login details. It also may increase OMSB service usage because users browsing the government portal will discover OMSB services listed there. The integration aligns with the **“no wrong door”** policy – regardless of whether a user goes to OMSB’s site or the main portal, they can access the service seamlessly. OMSB will coordinate with the Ministry of Communications and IT to ensure compliance with all requirements for integration (like security standards, UI guidelines for embedded services, etc.). By being present on the unified portal, OMSB reinforces that it is part of the whole-of-government digital ecosystem.

National Digital Identity & Beneficiary Data Hub

In **2026**, OMSB will connect its systems to the national **Beneficiary Data Hub** (often linked to the Civil Registration system). This integration allows OMSB’s online forms to automatically retrieve and validate basic personal data (such as an applicant’s name, Civil ID number, date of birth, etc.) directly from authoritative government sources. For example, when a doctor applies for a specialty program or an exam on the OMSB portal, they could input their Civil ID and the system auto-fills their name, address, etc., and confirms it against government records. This saves the user time (no need to re-enter data that government already has) and reduces errors (typos or inconsistent spelling). It also helps OMSB ensure data consistency and avoid duplicate records (since the national ID will serve as a unique key). Moreover, this link helps beyond applications - it could allow OMSB to get updates if a person’s status changes (like nationality, or if someone is reported deceased – ensuring OMSB doesn’t keep a license active wrongly). Integration with the ID hub thus improves data accuracy, user convenience, and identity verification, which is crucial for trust in digital transactions.

Government Integration Bus (National Integration Platform - NIP) & Shared Services

OMSB will leverage Oman’s centralized integration middleware (**NIP**) for any other cross-agency data exchanges. Instead of building custom point-to-point links with each entity, OMSB can use NIP’s standardized connectors. For instance:

  • To verify an applicant’s academic degrees, OMSB could integrate via **NIP** with the Ministry of Higher Education’s qualifications database (so that when someone applies for residency, their prior degrees are auto-verified online).
  • To share data on licensed specialists, OMSB can push that list to MoH’s licensing department or the central government manpower registry via **NIP**, rather than sending spreadsheets.
  • For payments, OMSB will use the **Government ePayment Gateway** for all online payments (ensuring secure transactions and common payment methods), rather than maintaining a separate payment system.
  • OMSB will use the **Government SMS Gateway** for sending official SMS notifications to users, which is cost-effective and ensures using official sender IDs.
  • For cloud hosting, OMSB will utilize the **Oman Government Cloud** infrastructure which provides a secure, government-approved environment for hosting applications (as already being done).
By **2026**, the aim is that all necessary routine data exchanges that used to be manual (letters, emails, excel sheets between agencies) are replaced by **API-based exchanges through NIP**. This not only speeds up processes but also meets government directives for integrated services. Essentially, OMSB will not “reinvent the wheel” for common services like payments or notifications – it will adopt the shared platforms available, which improves consistency and efficiency.

National Healthcare and Education Initiatives

OMSB will align and integrate with any major national initiatives in the healthcare or education sectors. For example, if Oman’s health authorities introduce a **National Health Workforce Planning System** or database, OMSB would contribute data (like number of graduating specialists per year, specialty distribution, etc.) to that system to aid planning. Or if a **National Continuing Education Platform** for all health professionals is established (perhaps to centralize CPD offerings across different councils), OMSB would integrate so that its CPD records are shared or its trainees can access materials on that platform. Similarly, OMSB will continue its practice of contributing to **open data portals** – publishing anonymized datasets such as number of residents in each specialty, pass rates, etc., to the national open data portal for transparency and to enable research. The integration mindset means OMSB regularly scans for new government projects (like if a new “Government AI Platform” is offered to agencies or a new version of the national ID system comes out) and ensures it hooks in as relevant. This guarantees that OMSB’s digital services remain compatible with the evolving digital government landscape.

Regional and International Integration

By **Phase 2 (2029+)**, OMSB will reach outward to integrate with regional platforms and counterpart institutions. For instance, OMSB will pursue interoperability with the **Saudi Commission for Health Specialties (SCFHS)** and other **GCC medical education/licensing bodies**. A practical outcome could be a **GCC-wide verification system** - if an Omani specialist wants to work in the UAE, the UAE’s system could ping OMSB’s database (through a regional network) to verify their certification instantly, and vice versa for other nationals coming to Oman. There are already moves in some regions to standardize such data exchange. OMSB can leverage organizations like the **Arab Board of Health Specializations** – by integrating with their database to auto-verify if someone has Arab Board certification when they apply to OMSB or to share Oman’s training info for regional recognition. On the educational side, integration with **international accreditation systems** could allow OMSB to benchmark or even get its programs recognized abroad. Another angle is participating in **global credential networks** (some groups work on blockchain-based global repositories for professional credentials). OMSB’s blockchain credentialing initiative would align well with joining such networks by 2030s. These integrations position OMSB as a globally connected entity, facilitating the mobility of Omani doctors and keeping Oman’s standards in sync with international best practices. It also plays into Oman’s soft power – showing leadership in adopting modern, internationally compatible systems.

Summary of Benefits

By implementing these integrations, OMSB will greatly enhance its service delivery and influence. **Stakeholders benefit from not having to shuttle between agencies; data entered in one place will serve multiple purposes.** For example, a trainee’s personal data gets verified from the national source once, then their certification can be verified by any hospital or foreign body without repeated paperwork – this streamlines career progression and licensing. Integration projects also directly correspond to key government excellence indicators (for instance, “integration with shared platforms” is a measured criterion), so OMSB’s score in those areas will improve.

Finally, integration ensures that OMSB’s contributions (data, services) amplify national efforts. For instance, making OMSB’s e-services accessible through the unified portal means more uptake and better public value. In essence, OMSB becomes an integral part of the **One-Government digital ecosystem**, rather than a siloed entity – which is exactly the vision of Oman’s e-government strategy and Oman Vision 2040’s call for collaborative, connected governance.

Four Pillars of National Data & Service Integration (Phase 1 Focus)

EHR (Nabd Al Shifa)

Technical Action (By 2027)

  • Secure, two-way data exchange.
  • Utilize HL7/FHIR APIs for real-time exchange.

Business Outcome

Link trainee logbooks to actual patient cases and automatically update MoH HR systems with verified certification status.

Result: Robust Clinical Verification & Workforce Planning
Unified Services Portal

Technical Action (By 2026)

  • Onboard all public-facing services (eligibility, process, fees).
  • Enable Single Sign-On (SSO) via national eID (Tam/PKI).

Business Outcome

Implements the "no wrong door" policy, improves user convenience, and drives service discovery/uptake.

Result: Seamless Access & Improved User Experience
Digital Identity Hub

Technical Action (By 2026)

  • Connect to National Beneficiary Data Hub (Civil Registration).
  • Use national ID as the unique key.

Business Outcome

Auto-validate and auto-fill basic applicant data, reducing errors and ensuring data consistency across all OMSB records.

Result: High Data Accuracy & Verification Confidence
NIP & Shared Services

Technical Action (By 2026)

  • Centralized integration via National Integration Platform (NIP).
  • Adopt Government ePayment and SMS Gateways.

Business Outcome

Replaces manual exchanges (Excel, letters) with API-based automation (e.g., auto-verifying degrees from MoHE). Avoids re-inventing common functions.

Result: Process Automation & Operational Efficiency
