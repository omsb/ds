Governance, Security, and Compliance

Robust governance and strong security are the cornerstones of this digital strategy, ensuring that initiatives are well-managed and risks are controlled. OMSB will institute multiple layers of oversight and align all activities with best practices and national regulations.

1. Digital Governance Structure

Steering Committee (8–10 members)

Chair: Her Excellency, the Executive President of OMSB

Purpose: Set strategic direction, ratify priorities and policies, approve funding, and track KPIs and risk. Reports to the OMSB Board.

Indicative Membership:

· Director of Education & Training

· Director of Examinations & Assessment

· Director of Accreditation & Quality

· Director of Information Technology

· Director of Finance & Administration

· Director of Human Resources & Organizational Development

· Director of Legal/Compliance

· Director of Communications & Public Relations

· (Ex-officio): CISO and CDO (when relevant)

Cadence: Monthly governance sessions, quarterly Board status, KPI dashboard, and risk register sign-off.

Program Leadership

Program Manager (Manager): Reports directly to the Steering Committee. Accountable for delivering the strategy, benefits realization, budget, schedule, risks, vendor management, and change enablement.

Assistant Manager: Reports to the Program Manager; manages day-to-day coordination, PMO routines, issue/action tracking, and dependency management across initiatives.

Digital Transformation Team

The core delivery team operating under the Program Manager. It comprises: Program Manager (lead), Assistant Manager, Community Manager, and Initiatives & Projects Managers (each owning a defined initiative).

Community Manager — Scope:
  • Own OMSB digital strategy brand, narrative, and style guide.
  • Run stakeholder awareness campaigns and publish service updates.
  • Lead change communications, feedback loops, and report sentiment/uptake metrics.

Initiatives & Projects — Scope

  • Each initiative has a Project/Initiative Manager (delivery) and a Business Owner.
  • Standard PMO controls: scope baselines, RAID logs, earned-value/velocity tracking, and benefits profiles with pre-defined KPIs.
  • Ways of Working: Weekly DT Team stand-up; bi-weekly initiative reviews; monthly Steering Committee.

4) Supporting Governance Boards (Decision Forums)

IT Governance & Architecture Board

Chair: Director of IT (with Enterprise Architect)

Mandate: Enterprise architecture standards, integration patterns, technology selections, change requests, exception handling.

Data Governance Committee

Chair: CDO (or delegated data steward)

Mandate: Data standards, quality issues, access/classification, open-data decisions, privacy impact assessments.

Information Security Oversight (CISO Function)

Lead: CISO

Mandate: ISMS/ISO 27001 controls, risk assessments, incident response approvals, MFA/privileged access, security by design.

5) Decision Rights & RACI (Summary)

Steering Committee: A/R for strategy, funding, portfolio priorities, major policy approvals, risk appetite.

Business Owners (Directors): A for business outcomes, process adoption, and operational handover.

Program Manager: A/R for portfolio delivery, budgets, cross-initiative dependencies, benefit tracking.

IT Gov & Arch Board: A/R for architecture standards, integrations, tech exceptions.

Assistant Manager / PMO: R for plans, reports, RAID, change logs, and cadence management.

Data Gov Committee: A/R for data standards, quality, and lawful use.

Initiative/Project Managers: R for scope/schedule/cost/quality at workstream level.

CISO Function: A/R for security controls and incidents.

Community Manager: R for communications, branding, awareness, and feedback channels.

Foundational Pillars

Policy Framework & Compliance

Goal: Near 100% compliance with Government's ICT controls by 2027.

  • National Policies (By 2026): Adopt the National Digital Accessibility Policy and an Internet Usage Policy.
  • Standards Alignment: Review/strengthen policies per ISO 27001 and Oman’s national cybersecurity framework.
  • Required Documentation: Information Security Governance Framework and ICT BCP/DRP by 2026.
  • Verification: Annual IT audits; supports Government Digital Transformation Excellence (40% institutional readiness weight).

Cybersecurity Enhancements

Goal: Achieve ISO 27001 certification by 2028.

  • ISMS: Implement an ISO/IEC 27001 aligned ISMS.
  • Access Control: RBAC, least privilege, MFA for admins & sensitive roles.
  • Data Protection: Encryption at rest and in transit (TLS everywhere).
  • Monitoring & Testing: Annual VA/PT and a 24/7 SOC capability by 2028.
  • Incident Readiness: Dedicated Incident Response Team & drills.
  • Privacy: Mandatory PIAs and alignment with evolving data protection regs.

Risk Management

Integrate digital transformation risks into the Enterprise Risk Management (ERM) framework.

  • Integration: Project, change, vendor, and cyber risks tracked under ERM.
  • Risk Register: Likelihood/impact, owners, and mitigations; reviewed every Steering Committee.
  • Mitigation Examples:
    • Change Resistance: Communications, training, end-user involvement.
    • Vendor Risks: SLAs, code escrow, knowledge transfer, fallback options.
    • Cybersecurity: Monitoring, firewalls, potential cyber insurance.
  • Contingency: Trigger extra resources and/or timeline adjustments if risks materialize.

Key Ways of Working & Transparency:

Cadence: Weekly DT Team stand-up; bi-weekly initiative reviews; monthly Steering Committee; quarterly Board review.

Controls: Standard PMO toolkit, stage gates, privacy/security by design, architecture compliance checks.

Transparency: Public roadmap highlights (where appropriate), service dashboards, change calendars.

Benefits: Initiative KPIs (SLAs, satisfaction, adoption) roll up to a benefits dashboard for the Steering Committee.

In summary, strong governance, rigorous security, and strict compliance are foundational to OMSB’s digital strategy. By setting up the right committees and policies, OMSB creates an environment where digital initiatives can be executed in a controlled, transparent manner. By hardening security and managing risks, OMSB protects its stakeholders and builds confidence in the digital services. These measures not only meet requirements but also instill a culture of accountability and safety, which is essential for sustainable digital transformation.
